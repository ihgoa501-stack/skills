# SWOT Analysis

**Origin:** Albert Humphrey / Stanford Research Institute (1960s–1970s)

**Core idea:** A structured assessment of internal and external factors affecting an organization, product, or person, to inform strategic decisions.

## The Matrix

| | **Helpful** (to the goal) | **Harmful** (to the goal) |
|---|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| **Internal** (within control) | **Strengths** | **Weaknesses** |
| **External** (outside control) | **Opportunities** | **Threats** |

## Questions to Ask

### Strengths (Internal + Helpful)
- What do we do well?
- What unique resources do we have?
- What do others perceive as our advantages?

### Weaknesses (Internal + Harmful)
- Where could we improve?
- What resources are we lacking?
- What do competitors do better?

### Opportunities (External + Helpful)
- What trends could benefit us?
- What market gaps exist?
- What technology changes open new doors?

### Threats (External + Harmful)
- What are competitors doing?
- What regulations could impact us?
- What market shifts could harm us?

## Action Planning

After the matrix, build strategies connecting the quadrants:

| Strategy | Approach |
|----------|----------|
| **SO** (Strengths → Opportunities) | Use strengths to seize opportunities |
| **WO** (Weaknesses → Opportunities) | Improve weaknesses to capture opportunities |
| **ST** (Strengths → Threats) | Use strengths to mitigate threats |
| **WT** (Weaknesses → Threats) | Defensive strategy — minimize exposure |

## When to Use
- Strategic planning or annual review
- Product launch assessment
- Personal career planning
- Competitive analysis
