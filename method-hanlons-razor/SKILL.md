# Hanlon's Razor (汉隆剃刀)

**Origin:** Robert J. Hanlon, popularized through Arthur Bloch's *Murphy's Law* series

## Core Principle

> **Never attribute to malice that which is adequately explained by neglect, incompetence, or misunderstanding.**

Most of the time, people aren't out to get you — they're just busy, distracted, confused, or overwhelmed.

## Why This Matters

| Assuming Malice | Assuming Error |
|-----------------|----------------|
| You're the victim → helplessness | It's process or communication → fixable |
| Escalates conflict | Invites clarification |
| Damages relationships | Builds understanding |
| Drains emotional energy | Preserves energy for solving problems |

## How to Apply

1. **Pause** — When you feel wronged, stop the initial emotional reaction
2. **Generate alternative explanations** — What else could explain this? (busy? misread? forgot? didn't know?)
3. **Assume the most charitable** — Start with the least hostile explanation
4. **Verify calmly** — Ask, don't accuse: "I noticed X — was that intentional or just timing?"
5. **Escalate only if proven** — If evidence of actual malice mounts, then address it

## Escalation Ladder

Don't jump from 1 to 10:

| Level | Explanation |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | They didn't know |
| 2 | They forgot |
| 3 | They misunderstood |
| 4 | They were overwhelmed |
| 5 | Systems/process failed |
| ... | ... |
| 9 | They intended harm but didn't think it through |
| 10 | Deliberate, calculated malice |

## Anti-Patterns

- ❌ **Ignoring real malice** — Some people *do* act maliciously. Don't be naive.
- ❌ **Passivity** — "It's just Hanlon's Razor" isn't an excuse to tolerate chronic incompetence
- ❌ **Using it on yourself** — Don't dismiss your own feelings: "they probably didn't mean it" doesn't make the impact disappear
- ❌ **Overcorrecting** — if the same person/pattern repeats 10 times, upgrade your assessment

## When to Use

- A colleague's email feels hostile — re-read it assuming they were in a hurry
- A feature you depend on broke — assume the other team didn't realize, not that they sabotaged you
- Someone didn't reply — they're busy, not ghosting you
- Organizational change feels targeted — it's usually systemic, not personal
