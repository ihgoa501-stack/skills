# Second-Order Thinking (二阶思维)

**Origin:** Howard Marks, William Poundstone, and general systems thinking — popularized by Warren Buffett and Charlie Munger

## The Concept

| Order | Question |
|-------|----------|
| **First-order** (obvious) | What happens immediately if I do X? |
| **Second-order** (deeper) | And then what happens because of that? |
| **Third-order** (deeper still) | And then what? |

## Classic Examples

| Action | First-Order | Second-Order | Third-Order |
|--------|------------|-------------|-------------|
| Subsidize grain | Farmers are happy | Overproduction → price collapse | Farmers worse off than before |
| Rent control | Tenants pay less | Landlords sell units | Housing shortage, tenants lose |
| Tax the rich more | Government gets revenue | Capital flight | Tax base shrinks, revenue drops |

## How to Apply

1. **Define the decision** and its immediate (first-order) consequence
2. **Ask "and then what?"** — map the chain 2–3 levels deep
3. **Identify feedback loops** — where do second-order effects amplify or counteract the first-order result?
4. **Consider time** — second-order effects often take longer to appear, creating the illusion that the first move worked
5. **Compare paths** — is the second/third-order outcome worth the first-order pain?

## Key Mental Models

- **Cobra effect** — incentives that make the problem worse (British India: bounty on cobras → people bred cobras → bounty cancelled → breeders released cobras → more cobras than before)
- **Goodhart's Law** — "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure"
- **Tragedy of the Commons** — individual rationality × collective irrationality

## Anti-Patterns

- ❌ **Paralysis** — over-analyzing 4th and 5th-order effects until you do nothing
- ❌ **Cynicism as thinking** — assuming everything leads to disaster isn't thinking deeper, it's just pessimism
- ❌ **Ignoring probability** — each order has lower certainty; weight them accordingly
- ❌ **First-order moralizing** — "this feels right/wrong" without checking downstream effects

## When to Use

- Major policy or strategic decisions
- Incentive design (compensation, pricing, regulation)
- Evaluating advice that sounds "obviously good"
- Investment decisions with long time horizons
