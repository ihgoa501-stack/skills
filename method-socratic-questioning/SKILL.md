# Socratic Questioning (苏格拉底提问法)

**Origin:** Socrates (469–399 BCE), as recorded by Plato — the teacher who never told, only asked

## The Six Types of Socratic Questions

| Type | Purpose | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| **1. Clarification** | Unpack vague statements | "What exactly do you mean by 'success'?" |
| **2. Probing Assumptions** | Expose hidden premises | "What are you assuming about the customer here?" |
| **3. Probing Evidence** | Demand justification | "What data supports this conclusion?" |
| **4. Alternative Perspectives** | Shift viewpoint | "What would a competitor say about this plan?" |
| **5. Implications & Consequences** | Follow the logic forward | "If we do this, what happens next? And then?" |
| **6. Questioning the Question** | Meta-level reflection | "Why are we asking this? What's the real concern?" |

## How to Apply

1. **Start with genuine curiosity** — You're exploring, not cross-examining
2. **One question at a time** — Don't stack questions; let each answer breathe
3. **Follow, don't lead** — Let their answers determine your next question
4. **Embrace silence** — People need time to think; don't fill the gap
5. **No "gotcha"** — The goal is clarity, not winning

## Application Formats

| Format | How |
|--------|-----|
| **Self-Socratic** | Write questions to your own plan/argument, answer in writing |
| **Dialogue** | Two people: one states, one questions |
| **Group** | Facilitator questions the group's consensus |
| **AI-augmented** | Prompt Claude to play Socrates against your proposal |

## Example Dialogue

```
Q: "We need to launch this feature by Q3."
A: "What happens if you don't?"
Q: "We'll lose market share to Competitor X."
A: "What evidence connects this feature to market share?"
Q: "... Actually, we assumed that. We haven't tested it."
A: "What's the smallest test you could run?"
```

## Anti-Patterns

- ❌ **Cross-examination mode** — Socratic questioning ≠ being a lawyer attacking a witness
- ❌ **Endless questioning** — Eventually you need to act. Question to decide, not to stall.
- ❌ **Fake ignorance** — "I'm just asking" when you're actually pushing an agenda
- ❌ **Targeting the person, not the idea** — The goal is stronger thinking, not humiliation

## When to Use

- Evaluating a strategic proposal with a lot of unexamined assumptions
- Team is rushing to consensus without digging into "why"
- Coaching/mentoring — help someone discover answers rather than being told them
- Your own thinking feels shallow — sit down and interrogate yourself
- Requirements gathering — "what do users need?" → "why do they need it?" → "is that really why?"
